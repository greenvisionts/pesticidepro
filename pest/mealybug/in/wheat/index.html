Mealybug in Wheat: Identification, Damage & Best Control Methods

What is Mealybug?

Mealybug (Phenacoccus solenopsis / Pseudococcus viburni) is a major sucking pest that causes significant yield losses in Wheat across India and globally. The pest causes damage by phloem sap sucking, sooty mold.

In India, Mealybug in Wheat is a serious problem in Punjab, Haryana, UP, MP. Left uncontrolled, it can cause 30-70% yield loss in severe infestations.

How to Identify Mealybug in Wheat

🔍 Key Symptoms on Wheat:

white cottony masses, stunted growth, honeydew secretion

Damage Pattern

The Mealybug is a sucking pest that damages Wheat through phloem sap sucking, sooty mold. Damage is most severe during Rabi season when conditions favour pest multiplication.

Spray Schedule for Mealybug in Wheat

When to Spray

Monitor your Wheat crop regularly. Spray when:

  • Economic Threshold Level (ETL) reached: 5-10 mealybug per leaf or shoot
  • First visible damage symptoms appear
  • During peak pest season for Wheat in your region
💡 Expert Tip: Spray in the early morning (before 9 AM) or late evening (after 5 PM) for best results. Avoid spraying during peak sunlight hours or when wind speed is above 8 km/h.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which is the best insecticide for Mealybug in Wheat?

Thiamethoxam 25% WG is highly effective. Apply at 0.3-0.5 ml/litre as a spray solution ensuring thorough leaf coverage. For severe infestations, use Buprofezin 25% SC as rotation. PHI: 5-7 days.

How to identify Mealybug damage in Wheat?

Look for: white cottony masses, stunted growth, honeydew secretion. Use a magnifying glass to spot the pest on leaf undersides. Compare with a Mealybug identification chart.

When to spray for Mealybug in Wheat?

Spray when infestation reaches ETL (Economic Threshold Level). For Wheat, this is typically 5-10 mealybug per leaf. During peak season (Rabi), monitor every 3-4 days.

Is Mealybug spreading to other crops near Wheat?

Yes. Mealybug can spread to neighbouring crops. If your Wheat is near Tomato, Cotton, Rice / Paddy, monitor those crops too. Create buffer zones with sticky traps to monitor spread.
